My training is in international business and management. My research experience spans corporate governance and nonmarket strategy, comparative institutional analysis across national contexts, and applied governance work inside industry.

The thread running through it is empirical rather than topical: **building credible, comparable measures from institutional and documentary material that was never produced for analysis**, and using them to examine how firms respond to the environments that constrain them.

I now work inside a company undergoing exactly the transition I find most consequential. As firms delegate significant decisions to algorithmic systems, the structures that govern them — boards, disclosure regimes, lines of accountability — remain calibrated to decisions made by people. That gap is where business, economics, and artificial intelligence stop being separate subjects, and it is the direction I intend to take into doctoral research.

I am currently seeking doctoral study in management and related fields.

## Method

Most of the work has been in measurement. The variables I have needed rarely existed in any database, and had to be built.

**Econometric analysis** — Panel fixed-effects models; instrumental-variable regression; propensity-score matching; fractional response and fractional multinomial logit models (QMLE); entropy balancing; control-function estimation of total factor productivity; observational research design and sensitivity diagnostics.

**Measurement construction** — Development of constructs unavailable in existing databases; codebook design; item-level document coding; inter-coder reliability analysis (Cohen's κ); pilot-first feasibility testing; explicit modelling of disclosure selection as a substantive rather than technical problem.

**Data architecture** — CSMAR; Wind Financial Terminal; CNINFO; corporate annual reports; national institutional and policy registries; structured web scraping and automated extraction with version-controlled pipelines and source-linked audit logs.

**Textual analysis** — Textual parsing; dynamic Chinese-language keyword dictionary construction validated against manual coding; bilingual policy, legal, and archival text analysis.

**Theoretical grounding** — Corporate governance and board composition; nonmarket strategy and the market–nonmarket tradeoff; authoritarian state–business relations; corporate information disclosure; comparative institutional analysis and the diffusion of organisational practice.
